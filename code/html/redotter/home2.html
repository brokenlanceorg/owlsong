Artist's Statement

The land of his youth is still much the same. But the stories, and faces, lovers, enemies, dreams, and visions of his youth are not found in the present.

There is an unspoken, never expressed longing in his heart to be in the ancient-time that the despairing Ghost Dancers sought; but this longing is tempered with the realization that it will never return. There is a hope for his children and his children's children that they will more fully belong to the modern ways.

"Waiting for Sno Cone Money"

It is a hard thing going through a time of transition. He remembers that his people prepared through Spring, Summer, and Fall to survive the winter.

This change of season for his family must be faced head-on and skillfully to meet this new time. The past lingers in memory but the modern age has arrived forever.

There are new stories and new people, new kinds of clothes, wooden beds and houses, sermons in church. He wears his white shirt and sits in a modern chair and keeps his hair braided.

And he keeps a bed in every room so each has a place to sleep when visiting. He sees his grandchildren now and then—fresh and happy, speaking English better than Kiowa and walking loudly and flat-footed in the rude and inelegant manner of Anglo children. He says nothing about it. What is there to say?

He sees that former times are, like him, aged and muted. So he keeps his hair braided and remembers.
