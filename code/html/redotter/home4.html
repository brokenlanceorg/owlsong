Artist's Statement

That he and some others of his generation were able to survive and create a new life is a testament to their adaptability and strength.

To find Tonemah's home, you must enter Kiowa Country in what is today Southwestern Oklahoma. When you leave Oklahoma City, where I was born, and travel South, turning West at Chickasha, you enter Kiowa Country.

Near Anadarko, Carnegie, and Hobart you will find yourself among the "Principal People"—our name for ourselves.

The Kiowa are noted in the historical literature as warlike people, Plains Indians who were adept at hunting buffalo and who lived out their nomadic conquests from the mountains of Montana to the middle of Mexico.

The Kiowa were so warlike that only a few survived and today they number only about 10,000.


"Deacon Tonemah"

Near the end of the 19th centry, the Kiowa could no longer hunt the buffalo because of the extermination of the herds. With the downfall of the buffalo—the center of Kiowa society and sustenance—the Kiowa entered a time of great change. Tonemah and his contemporaries were the last to live in the old world. To put it plainly, it would be like an ancient Roman traveling through time to visit modern New York.
