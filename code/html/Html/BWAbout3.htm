[The Philosophy Factor]
Shortly after I left the ‘Christian’ school, I read Will Durant’s “The Story of Philosophy”. My worldview was opened to such an extent that I felt I had been living in Plato’s cave. When I came to know Spinoza, I thought it was a matter of time before everything, including metaphysics, would fall to the command of rational thought. Spinoza seemed to display a sense of rigor most philosophers and theologians lacked. It is as Durant said: ”Spinoza is not to be read he is to be studied; you must approach him as you would approach Euclid, recognizing that in these brief two hundred pages a man has written down his lifetime’s thought with stoic sculptory of everything superfluous. Do not think to find it’s core by running over it rapidly…” For a fifteen year old, this kind of rigor seemed to be the sure sign of certainty. While I enjoyed the rigor afforded by an axiomatic approach, I still held the mathematicians in contempt because I believed mathematics to be largely human-made, and arbitrary.
I continued to read as many philosophical treatises as I could get my hands on. Then one day, while reading a Carl Jung work (I can’t recall exactly where now), Jung mentioned that the French mathematician Charles Hermite held that numbers exist in and of themselves. I thought about this for a second, and it blew my mind! It then occurred to me that maybe mathematicians weren’t actually inventing mathematics but rather discovering mathematics. I jumped into mathematics headfirst and I haven’t looked back since. Indeed, I consider myself of the ‘Platonist’ school, that is, the belief that mathematics exists ‘out there’ as an objective reality that we as humans perceive through rational thought. Thus, mathematical entities can be viewed by everyone (as theorems) and further that they can be viewed by different angles (as theorems in seemingly disparate fields).

[The Shire]
Behind my friend Varner’s house, a huge storage facility was built to store all of the chicken meat being produced by the nearby chicken plants. In the process of building this factory, a mound of useless debris and dirt was compiled. This pile of dirt proved a perfect place to conduct philosophical and theological discussions: to everyone except us, this pile of dirt was useless and abject. However, to me, Varner, and Conduff, this mountain was the perfect pinnacle to aspire, atop which we could soar into philosophical discussions of utmost importance.
