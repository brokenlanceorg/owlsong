[Brief Introduction
to Quantum Mechanics]

At the end of the 19th century, physicists were faced with solving the "Ultraviolet Catastrophe". This problem was so-named because at ultraviolet frequencies, classical theory and the emperica were in strong disagreement concerning black body radiation. In fact, as the frequency became larger, the discrepancy between predicted energy and actual energy became much larger.

Black body radiation is energy in the form of radiation that is emitted from a perfect absorber. An easy way to create a black body is to drill a small hole in the side of an oven. In this way, very little energy is absorbed from outside the oven, while most of the energy impinging on the hole will escape to the outside, i.e., the energy is emitted. The descrepancy between theory and experiment was considered a catastrophe because it showed that something was inherently wrong with the equations of classical thermodynamic theory.

Classical mechanics assumed the atoms to be vibrating in the cavity of the oven thus emitting electro-magnetic waves. From Maxwell's theory of electro-magnetism, one could calculate the number of 'standing wave' frequencies (N(n)dn) between the frequencies n and n + dn. Essentially, this is the number of oscillators, or particles, in the cavity vibrating within the frequency range n to n + dn. Now, if N(n)dn is multiplied by the average energy of an oscillator, an expression for the energy emitted by the black body within the range n to n + dn will be obtained.

To compute the average energy of a particle, an integral of probability-weighted energy is evaluated over all energies (a continuum of energies). The probability function is assumed to be a normalized Gaussian distribution function. When evaluated, this integral turns out to be kT, where k is Boltman's constant and T is the temperature of the cavity.
