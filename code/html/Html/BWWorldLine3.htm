Suppose an event occurs at d=2.5 (figure 3), we want to determine A's surface of simultaneity, that is to say, the line whose events all occur at the same time as the event at d=2.5 (blue dot). To do this, we send out a light signal towards the event (blue dot), the light will be relected back towards us (world-line A), when we receive the reflected signal, we calculate the total time, then divide by two in order to get the time at which our signal was reflected back to us (the event at the blue dot).


Figure 3.

This method may seem rather complicated, but it's required to find the surface of simultaneity for moving frames of reference.

For example, consider figure 4. In this image, another frame of reference (B) is moving towards A. Suppose B wants to find its surface of simultaneity with respect to the event at the blue dot. B sends out a light signal towards the blue dot. Since light travels at a constant velocity regardless of the frame of reference, the light ray's world-line is a 45 degree line. The light ray gets reflected back to B at the event and when B receives the reflected ray, we can calculate the midpoint between when B sent the light signal and when B received the light signal.
