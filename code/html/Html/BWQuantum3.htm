Even Maxwell predicted that electro-magnetic energy E would possess momentum: p = E / c, where c is the speed of light. Since the speed of light can be expressed in terms of its frequency, the speed can be written c = ln. Substituting into the equation of momentum yields: p = E / ln. Now, if we use Plank's hypothesis, E = hn, the momentum then becomes: p = hn / ln = h / l. Solving for the wavelength: l = h / p; thus an expression for the wavelength of the light is obtained. This result has also been verified experimentally.

Thus, it seems that light can behave like a particle p = h / l or as a wave l = h / p depending on how we want to view it.

Since light can behave like particles, could classical particles such as electrons and protons behave like waves? The idea that matter consists of 'matter waves' was treated in Louis de Broglie's 1924 doctoral thesis. This idea seems ludicrous at first glance, but take note of the minuteness of Plank's constant h = 6.626 x 10-34 Joule / sec.

This minuteness implies that for objects that have high velocities or masses, the matter wave is extremely small, however, for objects whose momentum is on the order of Plank's constant, the wave can become appreciable.

For example, an 800 kg car moving at 20 kg/sec would have an associated matter wave on the order of 4.1 x 10-28 Ångstroms (an Ångstrom is 10 x 10-10 of a meter), which is significantly smaller than the atomic nucleus; a proton with energy 1 keV would have a matter wave on the order of .009 Ångstroms, which is subatomic in size, while an electron with energy of 1 eV would have a matter wavelength of 12.3 Ångstroms, which is as large as some molecules.

How could matter waves be proven? One way, as de Broglie pointed out, is to get protons or electrons to interfere with each other to produce a diffraction pattern. Just as Young's double slit experiment showed the interference properties of light waves, one could use a double slit experiment to exhibit the interference properties of matter waves.
