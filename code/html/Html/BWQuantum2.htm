The problem is that kT doesn't cancel, or mitigate, the n2 term in the expression for N(n)dn. Thus, as n approaches infinity, N(n)dn * kT (equivalently, the energy in the range n to n + dn) approaches infinity! This result was completely unacceptable.

A paper published in the Annalen Der Physik (1900) by the physicist Max Plank at the University of Berlin resolved this quandary by assuming that the energy of an oscillator is restricted to certain multiples of its frequency, namely, En = nhn, where n is a positive integer and h is a constant.

Plugging this result back into the former calculations, we find that the integral for the average energy of a particle is no longer an integral, but rather a summation since the energy levels are no longer a continuum but a discrete set. This summation turns out to contain a fraction which has an exponential of the frequency in the denominator.

And, as any mathematics student knows, the exponential will 'overtake' the n2 term in N(n)dn for large n.

This new result for the energy agreed perfectly with experiment, and thus the quantum theory was born.

Little came of this new quantum theory for the next five years until, in 1905, Einstein used the quantum assumption to explain the photoelectric effect. The photoelectric effect was the phenomenon in which electrons were ejected when light was cast on a metal foil. So, it seemed that light was acting as billiard balls, with energy E = hn, knocking the electrons out. However, up to that point, most physicists had putatively agreed that light was a wave.

Young's famous double slit experiment demonstrated light's wave-like behavior, yet with Einstein's result, light appeared to act as particles. The strange thing about all this is that light behaves differently depending on how we look at the light.
