The walls of the medium (axon or dendrite) are made of proteins which can function as membranous gates allowing certain ions through, while rejecting others. The medium can then modify its gates down its length, which causes certain ions to move in and others out, and consequently, and impulse to travel down its length. This process is indeed considerably slower than the metal wire scenario, however, due to this regenerative action down the length of the axon, the impulse spike is able to maintain its initial strength.

Most axons are several feet long, some can be up to ten feet or more in length¾how else would one twitch one’s toes! Research has shown that if the impulses incoming from the dendrites are above a certain threshold, then the neuron outputs an impulse down its axon, whereupon the impulse is received into other dendrites through synaptic clefts. The nature of this impulse down the axon is a property of brain function that interested Helmholtz; he began studying them in 1852. He discovered that the wider the axon, the faster the impulse signal could travel down its length. As an aside, some of the axons he studied in roaches were several magnitudes larger than any found in humans.

Helmholtz performed most of his experiments on what are known as unmyelinated axons; however, most axons found in humans are coated with a growth of cells called the myelin sheath. This sheath functions as an insulator, thereby improving the impulse’s speed by decreasing the pernicious effects of “loss of signal”. With m being a thousandth of a millimeter, typical axon widths range from .5m up to 10m. Helmholtz discovered that the average speed of these impulses was about five feet per second, which was drastically smaller in magnitude than what was previously held¾for it was putatively held that the impulses traveled at the speed of light.

Axons rarely attach directly to a neuron, rather, they attach to dendrites. This junction is known as the synaptic cleft. Similarly, the axon does not attach itself directly to the dendrite, but it is separated by the cleft’s distance of about .05m. When the impulse reaches the synaptic cleft, proteins called vesicles are released which trigger certain gates on the post-synaptic side (dendritic side), which allow the necessary ions to flow in or out.
