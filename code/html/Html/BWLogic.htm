The science of logic is generally acredited to Aristotle on basis of his Organon (Instrument of Reasoning, c300 B.C.). In this work, the venerated Aristotelian logic was created, where the law of excluded middle may be found: every meaningful statement is either true or false. Aristotle's work consisted mainly of syllogisms.

It was Gottfried Willhem Leibniz (1646-1716) and René Decartes (1596-1650) who envisioned a more powerful logical system than the stagnating system inherited from the ancient Greeks.

Leibniz envisioned a Universal Characteristic within which all statements could be formulated and subsequently answered; at the time, it was believed that any well-formed statement in such a formal system could be proved either true or false. Thus, through applied logic, Leibniz could construct models of reality which could be proved to be correct.

We know today, thanks to the Incompleteness Theorems of Kurt Gödel, that no such system can ever exist. Despair need not set in, for mathematics and logic, with great success, can be applied to endless problems whose solutions would have otherwise been intractable.

Consider wave-like phenomena. This type of behavior can be modeled through the application of logic and mathematics. Additionaly, the derivation of the wave-like system is quite beautiful.
